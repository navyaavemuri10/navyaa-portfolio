The problem

Most payment tools can tell you something failed. They rarely help you resolve it.

When I started exploring payments workflows, I noticed a pattern: most products were pretty good at showing status, but weak at guiding recovery. You could see that a payment was pending, failed, or returned — but once something went wrong, the product usually stopped being helpful.

That felt like a missed opportunity, especially in operational workflows. A payments ops analyst is not just looking at transactions for awareness. They’re trying to answer much more urgent questions: what failed, why did it fail, and what should happen next?

Good at showing status. Bad at guiding recovery.

Where they fall short

Research

I started from the consumer layer, then translated those gaps into an operations use case.

Because real internal banking ops dashboards aren’t publicly visible, I looked at the systems people actually interact with: Chase, Wells Fargo, and Revolut. I wasn’t trying to copy their UI. I was trying to understand how current products communicate transaction status, failure, and recovery — and where those systems break down.

Research approach
I studied consumer-facing payment flows to identify patterns in status language, failure handling, receipt visibility, and recovery paths, then reframed those findings for a payments operations analyst managing issues at scale.
What I consistently saw
Pending, completed, and failed states were visible
Some systems exposed basic detail views or receipts
Users could sometimes retry, but rarely with guidance
High-level history was usually easy to scan
What was missing
Few products explained why failures happened in context
Recovery was often pushed into support or email
No clear “needs attention” layer existed
There was little help deciding what action to take next

The user
I defined the primary user as a Payments Operations Analyst — someone responsible for reviewing failed or at-risk transactions, understanding what went wrong, and moving them toward resolution.
Design principles

This needed to feel like an operations tool, not an analytics dashboard.

One of the biggest clarifying moments in the project was realizing I was not designing a payment analytics dashboard. I was designing a resolution system. That changed the way I approached the entire experience.

Observation-only tools
Show trends, charts, and performance metrics, but stop short of helping someone resolve an individual issue.
My direction
Design for action. Every part of the interface should help analysts identify, understand, and resolve problematic transactions.
Flat transaction lists
Force users to manually scan everything and decide what matters.
My direction
Prioritize before presenting. Add a clear “Needs Attention” layer so urgency is visible before the table even starts.
Generic failure states
Tell users something broke without helping them decide what to do next.
My direction
Separate issue, recommendation, and timeline. Each piece answers a different question and reduces decision fatigue.
Design

I designed the system in three layers: prioritize, investigate, resolve.

1. Needs Attention: adding a layer of urgency above the table

The first major design move was introducing a prioritization layer. Instead of making analysts scan a full table to figure out what mattered, I surfaced the highest-priority categories first: Failed, Delayed, Multiple failures, and High-value.

This mattered because not every problem in a payment system looks the same. A delayed transaction is not the same as a failed one. A high-value transaction may still be pending, but it deserves attention for a different reason. I separated these into derived categories so the interface could highlight urgency without overloading the core status model.

Key design decision
I treated Needs Attention as a prioritization layer, not a status layer. The table still shows raw system states like pending or failed, while the cards help analysts interpret what needs attention first.

2. The table: built for scanning, not reading

The table had to feel dense without becoming overwhelming. I prioritized columns that directly support triage: Status, Recipient, Payment Method, Amount, Issue, and Activity. I intentionally avoided turning it into a database dump.

Two decisions mattered most here. First, I added an Issue column with short, human-readable labels instead of burying failure causes deeper in the flow. Second, I combined dates into a single Activity column so analysts could quickly see both freshness and context without sacrificing space.

3. The side panel: turning failure into a decision-making workflow

The side panel became the core of the experience. This is where I stopped thinking about “details” and started thinking about decision support. Once an analyst clicks a row, they need four things immediately: what happened, why it happened, what should happen next, and what’s already happened in the system.

To make that possible, I broke the panel into distinct layers: issue explanation, a recommended action, a compact timeline, and desktop-native actions that move the transaction forward without leaving the workflow.

Recommendation logic
I framed recommendations as a mix of system rules and historical patterns, so they felt trustworthy rather than magical. Instead of vague advice, the interface suggests a concrete next step and supports it with lightweight confidence signals from similar resolved cases.
Prototype

I prototyped the system like a workflow, not a slideshow.

Once the core UI was in place, I focused on the small interactions that make the system feel real. Clicking a Needs Attention card filters the table and explains why those transactions were surfaced. Clicking a row opens the side panel. Clicking Request update triggers a confirmation state and removes the row from the active queue, which makes resolution feel like a true state transition instead of a fake button click.

My next step is to build this in code using Tailwind and AI-assisted tools so I can push the interaction model further and make the prototype feel even closer to a real product.

Outcome

The final concept closes the gap between insight and action.

The biggest shift in this project was moving from a passive transaction table to a real operational workflow. Instead of asking analysts to interpret a long list of statuses on their own, the system now helps them see what matters, understand what happened, and take the next appropriate step without leaving the interface.

Learnings

This project reminded me that systems matter more than single screens.

01
Designing for experts doesn’t mean removing guidance. Ops analysts do not need hand-holding, but they do need clean, well-structured decision support. The challenge was figuring out how to guide without over-explaining.
02
Status is not the same thing as urgency. One of the strongest ideas in this project was separating raw system states from derived priority categories. That distinction made the whole interface clearer.
03
The most valuable design move was connecting insight to action. A dashboard alone would not have been enough. The project became more compelling once it supported the full flow from triage to investigation to resolution.
