The problem

Most payment tools can tell you something failed. They rarely help you resolve it.

When I started exploring payments workflows, I noticed a pattern pretty quickly: most products were good at surfacing transaction status, but weak at guiding recovery. You could see that a payment was pending, failed, or returned — but once something went wrong, the product usually stopped being helpful.

That felt especially limiting in operations workflows. A payments ops analyst is not just looking at transactions for awareness. They are trying to answer more urgent questions: what failed, why did it fail, and what should happen next?

Research

I started by understanding the job itself before I designed any screens.

Because I was designing for a user I had not worked with directly before, I first needed to understand what a payments operations analyst actually does. I researched the role, studied adjacent payments products, and mapped out a simple operational flow: monitor, investigate, decide, act.

I also used a quick storyboard and user flow to pressure-test the scenario itself before moving into interface design. That helped me avoid generic “failed payment” UI and instead focus on the analyst’s actual function: triaging unclear issues, understanding what happened, and choosing the right next step.

Research approach
I studied consumer-facing payment flows like Chase, Wells Fargo, and Revolut to understand how current systems communicate status, failure, and recovery, then translated those gaps into an operations use case.

Design principles

This needed to feel like an operations tool, not an analytics dashboard.

One of the biggest clarifying moments in the project was realizing I was not designing a payment analytics dashboard. I was designing a resolution system. That shifted the whole project from passive visibility to decision support.

Observation-only tools

My direction

Design

I designed the system in three layers: prioritize, investigate, resolve.

Full dashboard overview with Needs Attention cards, filters, search, and transaction table.

1. Needs Attention: adding a layer of urgency above the table

The first major move was introducing a prioritization layer. Instead of making analysts scan a full table to figure out what mattered, I surfaced the highest-priority categories first: Failed, Delayed, Multiple failures, and High-value.

This mattered because not every problem in a payment system looks the same. A delayed transaction is not the same as a failed one. A high-value transaction may still be pending, but it deserves attention for a different reason. I separated these into derived categories so the interface could highlight urgency without overloading the core status model.

Key design decision
I treated Needs Attention as a prioritization layer, not a status layer. The table still shows raw system states like pending or failed, while the cards help analysts interpret what needs attention first.
One filtered state showing how clicking a Needs Attention card changes the table and explains why those rows were selected.

2. The table: built for scanning, not reading

The table had to feel dense without becoming overwhelming. I prioritized columns that directly support triage: Status, Recipient, Payment Method, Amount, Issue, and Activity. I intentionally avoided turning it into a database dump.

Two decisions mattered most here. First, I added an Issue column with short, human-readable labels instead of burying failure causes deeper in the flow. Second, I combined dates into a single Activity column so analysts could quickly see both freshness and context without sacrificing space.

3. The side panel: turning failure into a decision-making workflow

The side panel became the core of the experience. This is where the project stopped being a detail view and became a resolution workflow. Once an analyst clicks a row, they need four things immediately: what happened, why it happened, what should happen next, and what has already happened in the system.

To make that possible, I broke the panel into distinct layers: issue, recommendation, timeline, and action controls. That separation helped the UI stay compact while still supporting high-stakes decision-making.

Recommendation logic
I framed recommendations as a mix of system rules and historical patterns, so they felt trustworthy rather than magical. Instead of vague advice, the interface suggests a concrete next step and supports it with lightweight confidence signals from similar resolved cases.
Prototype

I prototyped the experience like a workflow, not a slideshow.

Once the core UI was in place, I focused on the small interactions that make the system feel real. Clicking a Needs Attention card filters the table and explains why those transactions were surfaced. Clicking a row opens the side panel. Clicking Request update triggers a confirmation state and removes the row from the active queue, which makes resolution feel like a true state transition rather than a fake button click.

My next step is to build this in code using Tailwind and AI-assisted tools so I can push the interaction model further and make the prototype feel even closer to a real product.

Outcome

The final concept closes the gap between insight and action.

The biggest shift in this project was moving from a passive transaction table to a real operational workflow. Instead of asking analysts to interpret a long list of statuses on their own, the system now helps them see what matters, understand what happened, and take the next appropriate step without leaving the interface.

Learnings

This project reminded me that systems matter more than single screens.

01
Designing for experts doesn’t mean removing guidance. Ops analysts do not need hand-holding, but they do need clean, well-structured decision support. The challenge was figuring out how to guide without over-explaining.
02
Status is not the same thing as urgency. One of the strongest ideas in this project was separating raw system states from derived priority categories. That distinction made the whole interface clearer.
03
The most valuable move was connecting insight to action. A dashboard alone would not have been enough. The project became much stronger once it supported the full flow from triage to investigation to resolution.
