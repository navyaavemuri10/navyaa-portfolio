Problem

Students did not trust the website, so they stopped using it.

Despite serving a campus of 23,000 students, the portal had only 13,003 engaged sessions across the year before the redesign. Many students would rather walk into the health center than rely on the website for basic information.

That told me the real issue was not just navigation. It was credibility.

Role

I owned the redesign end to end over two years.

I was the first dedicated UX designer in the department. That meant I was not just designing screens. I was building the team's design approach from scratch while improving a product students actually depended on.

Strategy
Prioritized journeys, mapped information architecture, and identified the pages causing the most friction.
Design
Designed new page structures, reusable modules, clearer calls to action, and more trustworthy visual systems.
Delivery
Worked directly within Drupal constraints, coordinated with stakeholders, and iterated through analytics once changes were live.
2023
Audited the existing site, interviewed staff, and identified trust and navigation as the core problems.
2024
Rolled out new structures, reusable patterns, and clearer paths for appointments, resources, and student support.
2025
Kept refining the system through analytics, campaign work, and long-term iteration across the portal.
Two years of continuous ownership gave me room to design, ship, measure, and improve instead of stopping at a concept.
Constraints

Modern UX inside a 2011 CMS.

The portal ran on Drupal 7. Even simple design changes were difficult. Instead of waiting for a perfect rebuild, I focused on what I could control: hierarchy, clarity, trust, and consistency.

Constraint
No modern component system
Limited layout flexibility
Weak responsive control
Content and SEO limitations
My response
Built consistency through reusable design patterns
Prioritized desktop where 75% of traffic existed
Preserved SEO with native text where it mattered
Focused first on the highest-intent pages
Why that mattered
This project was not about waiting for ideal conditions. It was about making a better product with the system that already existed.
Design

I rebuilt trust through clarity.

I was not trying to make the site prettier. I was trying to make students feel sure they were in the right place. That meant clearer paths, fewer dead ends, stronger hierarchy, and language that felt human instead of institutional.

Basic Needs
Task-based structure Flatter navigation Single source of truth
The biggest shift was structural. I moved the portal from a department-based system to a task-based one that surfaced what students were actually trying to do.
Reusable modules helped create consistency across a legacy system that did not support modern components.
Three design principles guided the work
Make actions obvious. Keep information scannable. Help the site feel human enough to trust.
That showed up in clearer booking paths, stronger hierarchy, warmer language, and fewer places to get lost.
Stakeholder challenge
Some stakeholders wanted pages with more text because the information felt important. Analytics showed the opposite. Dense pages were making students leave. I used data and prototypes to move the conversation away from opinion and toward outcomes.
Results

The site started acting like a real source of truth.

Within the first full year after the redesign, engaged sessions grew from 13,003 to 102,652. Drop-off fell 60% across redesigned pages. By year three, engagement had surpassed 200,000 sessions.

Product impact
Students could find information faster and trust that they were in the right place.
Operational impact
The site answered more routine questions, reducing unnecessary back and forth with staff.
Long-term impact
The design system and structure kept scaling beyond the first launch.
Outcome
Engaged sessions over three years
From 13,003 at baseline to 200,000+ confirmed. This was not a one-time spike. It kept growing.
Learnings

What this project says about how I work.

01
I simplify messy systems. This project was not just about screens. It was about untangling a broken structure and making it easier for people to trust.
02
I use data without losing empathy. The metrics told me where things were failing. The student experience told me why that failure mattered.
03
I can ship inside constraints. Drupal 7 was limiting, but it did not stop the work from getting better. It just forced sharper decisions.
