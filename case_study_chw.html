Context

I was Cal Poly's first dedicated UX designer — brought in to fix a health portal students had quietly stopped trusting.

The site existed. Students just weren't using it. They'd walk in and ask staff for things the website should have answered — hours, forms, how to book an appointment.

User & Org Problem

Students didn't trust the site, so they avoided it entirely.

For students
Finding basic health info felt unreliable. Students couldn't trust what they found, so they gave up.
For the org
Staff spent time answering questions the site should handle. A health resource no one uses isn't just a UX problem — it's a care gap.

Solution

Rebuild trust through clarity.

Not a redesign for aesthetics — a redesign for credibility.

I audited the existing site, identified the specific points of failure, and systematically rebuilt the IA, visual hierarchy, and content structure from inside a deeply constrained CMS.

Identifying Pain Points

I combed through every page before touching a single design.

Dead links, redundant content, buried actions — I catalogued everything costing students their trust. Three buckets kept coming up:

I cross-referenced my audit against Google Analytics — session data, drop-off rates, bounce by page — to confirm the issues I was seeing in the UI showed up in the numbers too. They did.

I also went off-screen: ran in-person booths and collaborated with student-run org Pulse on surveys around STI resources and sexual health — the pages students were least likely to navigate to alone.

Constraints

Modern UX inside a 2011 CMS #drupal7

Drupal 7 was limiting in ways that made every design decision harder. No component system. Limited layout control. I couldn't wait for a rebuild — I had to make the existing system better.

[ Figma design file ]

Designed and maintained everything in Figma first to keep visual consistency the CMS couldn't enforce on its own.

[ Drupal implementation ]

Implemented directly in Drupal with proper alt text to maintain SEO without relying on modern tooling.

Key tradeoff: I designed with desktop responsiveness but not mobile — 82% of users were on desktop, so mobile was deprioritized by data, not by default.

Design System

How to build trust

Started with IA. Before screens, I mapped the full site — what existed, what was duplicated, what was missing. The biggest shift: department-based → task-based.

Before — department-based

Then I built a design system to hold it together — reusable modules, consistent type scale, color tokens — so a CMS with no component support could still feel coherent.

Putting It Together

Designing the pages

Three principles guided every page: make actions obvious, keep content scannable, make the site feel human enough to trust.

Some stakeholders pushed for denser pages — "the information feels important." Analytics showed the opposite: dense pages had the highest drop-off. I used session data and prototypes to move the conversation from opinion to outcomes.

Results

The site started acting like a real source of truth.

In year one, engaged sessions grew from 13,003 to 102,652. By year three, they'd passed 200,000. It wasn't a spike — it kept compounding.

Learnings

01
Messy systems are a design problem too.
Half the work was untangling broken structure before I touched a screen. Clarity at the system level made everything downstream easier.
02
Data and empathy aren't opposites.
Metrics told me where things were failing. Student behavior told me why it mattered. I needed both to make a case and make it stick.
03
Constraints sharpen decisions.
Drupal 7 forced me to be deliberate about every choice. The limitation wasn't the problem — it was the design brief.
