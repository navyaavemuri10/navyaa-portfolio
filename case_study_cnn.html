Opportunity 01 · Election Center

Designed for evolving information needs as uncertainty decreased and urgency increased.

The Election Center was not a single static page. It had to evolve with the election cycle. Early coverage needed context and education. Closer to election night, audiences needed speed, comparison, and a clear path to results.

I explored layouts and information hierarchy across breakpoints, presenting iterations to product leaders and engineers while balancing user needs, editorial priorities, business goals, and delivery timelines.

Early cycle
Prioritized context, candidate information, and issue education for users still forming understanding.
Closer to election day
Shifted emphasis toward comparison, timeliness, and faster paths to the most relevant updates.
Election night
Explored results-first hierarchy for users who needed clarity quickly under high uncertainty.
Earlier cycle exploration: education-first hierarchy for users still learning candidates, issues, and context.
Later cycle exploration: results-first hierarchy for users looking for quick answers and direct comparison.
Design intent
Use information hierarchy to match changing user intent
Support scanning patterns with clear modules and visual weight
Balance editorial storytelling with source-of-truth data
Make candidate and issue comparison easier to process
What I learned
Information needs change as public uncertainty changes
Mobile hierarchy needs to carry the entire experience
High-stakes design requires faster stakeholder alignment
Not every strong design ships exactly as imagined
Mobile-first exploration showing how the Election Center hierarchy adapts across breakpoints.
What actually shipped
My designs did not ship directly, but the process taught me how editorial priorities, engineering constraints, business timelines, and user needs shape high-stakes product decisions. I learned to present rationale clearly, defend decisions with structure, and keep iterating without taking changes personally.
Opportunity 02 · Smart TV banner

Made election information accessible from across the room.

The Smart TV banner had a unique constraint: people were not reading from a phone or laptop. They were sitting across the room. That meant the message had to be readable at a distance, easy to act on, and accessible even if the QR code was not usable.

I designed a concise hierarchy with one clear header, one supporting line, a QR code, and a URL fallback so viewers had more than one way to continue.

Smart TV election banner with content hierarchy optimized for distance readability and action.
Constraint
TV audiences scan from farther away, so copy had to be short, clear, and high contrast.
Decision
Added both QR and URL pathways so access did not depend on a single interaction pattern.
Learning
Good content design changes depending on context, distance, device, and user attention.
Opportunity 03 · Content systems

Identified opportunities to strengthen content consistency across CNN products.

I did not own or create CNN’s full content design guidelines. My role was to audit, research, and recommend ways to make content decisions more consistent across product surfaces.

I interviewed internal users, reviewed existing patterns, analyzed competitor systems, and synthesized recommendations around hierarchy, readability, calls-to-action, and content structure. The work helped inform broader conversations about how teams could align around clearer standards.

Competitor analysis used to identify patterns across content design guidelines, documentation systems, and editorial product surfaces.
What I did
Interviewed 9 internal users across 7 departments
Audited content patterns across CNN product surfaces
Reviewed direct and indirect competitors
Synthesized recommendations for consistency and usability
What I recommended
Clearer content hierarchy across surfaces
Reusable guidance for CTAs and supporting copy
Stronger examples of dos, don'ts, and use cases
More consistent structures for team-facing documentation
Why this mattered
Content consistency is not just a writing problem. It affects how quickly teams move, how clearly users understand information, and how trustworthy a product feels across surfaces.
Reflection

This internship taught me how to design when information, stakes, and timelines are all moving fast.

CNN pushed me to think about content design as product design. Whether I was exploring election modules, writing for Smart TV, or identifying content system gaps, the work came back to the same question: what does the user need to understand right now?

01
Information needs are temporal. The right hierarchy changes as the user's urgency, context, and uncertainty change.
02
Trust comes from clarity. In high-stakes products, clear structure helps users focus on what matters instead of fighting the interface.
03
Content design is systems design. Copy, hierarchy, documentation, and interaction patterns all shape how people understand and act on information.
