The problem

A newly integrated team with no shared content system.

CNN's content design team had been newly integrated, but there was no centralized guidance for voice, hierarchy, or how content should behave across surfaces. Different teams were solving the same problems in different ways.

My job was to define what strong content design looked like at CNN, document it, and help the team move faster with a common language.

What was breaking down
Inconsistent voice across teams and platforms
Duplicated work and slow review cycles
No onboarding resource for new contributors
Fragmented user experience across surfaces
The constraints
11-week internship with election coverage approaching
9 internal users across 7 departments to align
Needed to balance editorial and product priorities
Guidelines had to scale across web, mobile, and TV
Research

9 users. 7 departments. 2 weeks.

I interviewed internal stakeholders across CNN Digital because the success of the work depended on the people who would actually use it. The goal was to uncover what standards were missing, where misalignment happened, and what would make a shared system stick.

Competitor analysis comparing direct and indirect approaches to content design guidance.

Key decision
Rather than document everything, I focused on the highest-frequency content decisions people were making inconsistently. Voice and tone came first because they touched every surface.
Election Center

A living product that changed with the election timeline.

The Election Center was never one fixed design. It evolved week by week as user needs shifted from context and education to speed, comparison, and results. I iterated on hierarchy and layout while presenting the work to engineers and product leaders to align on user-centered changes.

Early cycle: more contextual and education-first for users still learning the landscape.
Election night: faster, denser, and built around definitive answers.
Design intent
Use video placement to support scanning behavior and business goals
Shift grid density as users move from discovery to comparison
Group objective financial data to balance campaign-heavy content
Why it mattered
Users needed different information at different moments
The layout had to support both exploration and rapid retrieval
Trust depended on hierarchy holding up under pressure
Mobile-first thinking ensured the same content priorities held up across breakpoints.
What actually shipped
My designs did not ship pixel-for-pixel, but that taught me something more valuable: in newsroom environments, final work is shaped by editorial priorities, engineering constraints, timing, and multiple stakeholders. I learned how to defend rationale, adapt quickly, and not equate authorship with impact.
Smart TV banner

One banner in millions of living rooms.

For CNN's Smart TV election banner, I focused on content that would motivate action without overwhelming viewers: one clear headline, one supporting line, a QR code, and a URL. The goal was to create urgency and clarity in a surface people would only glance at briefly.

Banner breakdown showing both the messaging rationale and the shipped TV experience.
Learnings

What high-stakes, high-speed design taught me.

01
Content design is systems design. The hardest part was not writing rules, but structuring them so people would actually use them.
02
Speed and quality need a system underneath them. Iteration only works when the team has a shared frame for making decisions.
03
The best copy disappears into behavior. The Smart TV banner worked when people simply knew what to do next.
complexity is easy but clarity takes work.
