The problem

A newly integrated team. No shared standards. An election in three months.

CNN's content design function had just been integrated across the organization, but without centralized guidelines or standards. Different teams were using different voices, different visual approaches, and different content hierarchies depending on which product they owned. During a normal news cycle, this created friction. During presidential election coverage, it was a genuine risk.

My job: figure out what good content design looked like at CNN, synthesize it from research and evidence, and build a document that the whole organization could actually use.

What no guidelines meant
Inconsistent voice and tone across platforms
Duplicated effort with no shared decisions to reference
Slow review and approval cycles across teams
No onboarding resource for new designers or writers
The real constraints
11 weeks before the election coverage peak
9 users across 7 departments to align and interview
Editorial voice had to coexist with product needs
Guidelines needed to work across web, mobile, and TV
Research

9 users. 7 departments. 2 weeks. 30 minutes each.

I interviewed CNN digital employees across multiple teams and roles: the people who actually created, reviewed, and published content every day. Internal user research was the only research that mattered here. Generic content design best practices were not the problem. What CNN teams actually did, said, and argued about in practice was the problem.

I supplemented the interviews with competitive analysis of peer organizations' content systems and a review of every piece of existing internal documentation I could find, including archived style references that predated the team integration.

Content guidelines

CNN's first content design guidelines. Built from interviews, not assumptions.

I ran card sorting exercises to prioritize what to tackle first, focusing on the decisions teams were making most frequently and most inconsistently. Voice and tone came first because it affected every piece of content across every surface. Information hierarchy came second because it affected every layout decision across web, mobile, and TV.

The document was built to be used by teams who were already busy, not read like a style manual. Every recommendation linked directly to the research finding that motivated it. Decisions were explained, not just prescribed.

The conflict

Business goals and user needs pulled in different directions. I had to hold both.

The central tension
CNN had commercial and editorial priorities tied to specific stories and partnerships. Users needed fast, clear access to what mattered based on where they were in the election timeline. These were not always the same thing.
Business priority
High-engagement editorial content and partnership placements at the top. More prominent = more valuable.
User behavior data
Early in the cycle, users skipped partnership content to find candidate context. Engagement data justified hierarchy changes in weekly reviews.
Closer to election night
Some stakeholders wanted to preserve earlier sections that were no longer driving engagement.
My approach
Tracked which sections users engaged with at each phase. Presented data in every weekly review. When evidence was specific enough, disagreements resolved.
Election Center

A product that had to change shape as the election evolved.

The Election Center was not a single design. It was a living product that needed different information architecture at different points in the election timeline. I iterated on it weekly throughout my internship, presenting to engineering and product leadership to maintain alignment and build buy-in for each round of changes.

The core design challenge was flexibility. The page hierarchy that served users in August (educational, contextual, exploratory) was wrong for users on election night (fast, results-focused, definitive). I designed the architecture to accommodate both modes without rebuilding the page from scratch for each shift.

Smart TV banner

One banner. Millions of living rooms. No second chances.

I designed the audience-focused messaging for CNN's Smart TV election banner by working backward from behavior: what would actually make someone in their living room pick up their phone and scan a QR code? The answer was not more information. It was the right information in the right order, with enough personal relevance to motivate action.

The structure I landed on: a single clear header that set expectations, one line of body copy that created personal connection and implied convenience, and a URL as an accessible fallback for users who would not scan a code. Simple. Specific. Invisible when it worked.

Learnings

What high-stakes, high-speed design at a major news organization actually teaches you.

01
Content design is systems design. Building guidelines is an information architecture problem before it is a writing problem. The hardest part was not writing the rules. It was deciding what order to present them in, how to structure the document, and what evidence to include so teams would actually trust and use it.
02
Speed and quality are compatible when the system is right. Rapid iteration on the Election Center worked because I had a clear framework for what we were optimizing at each phase of the election timeline. Without that framework, iteration is just guessing faster. Building the system first is what makes the speed possible.
03
The best copy disappears into behavior. Success on the Smart TV banner meant a viewer did not consciously read the copy. They just scanned the code. Design that makes people act without noticing they were persuaded is harder to make than design that calls attention to itself. That bar shaped everything I wrote that summer.
"complexity is easy. clarity takes work."
