The problem

A newly integrated team with no standardized guidelines.

CNN's content design team had just been integrated across the organization without centralized guidelines or standards. Different teams used different voices, visual approaches, and content hierarchies, which led to fragmented internal and external user experiences.

My job was to figure out what good content design looked like at CNN, document it, and help the team move faster with a shared language.

No guidelines meant
Inconsistent voice across platforms
Duplicated effort across teams
Slow review and approval cycles
No onboarding resource for new team members
The constraints
11-week timeline with election coverage looming
9 users across 7 departments to align
Balancing editorial voice with product needs
Guidelines had to work across web, mobile, and TV

Election Center

A living product. Iterated weekly through the entire election cycle.

The Election Center wasn't a single design but a product that changed shape as the election timeline evolved. I iterated on layouts and information hierarchy weekly, presenting to engineers and product leaders to build buy-in for user-centered changes while balancing business objectives.

The core challenge was that what users needed to know shifted as the election got closer. Early coverage needed context and education. Closer to election night, it needed speed and results.

Early cycle. Education-first, contextual, built for users still learning the candidates and issues.
Election night. Results-first, fast, built for users who need a single definitive answer.
Design intent
Video content on the far right to align with the F-pattern of scanning
Weighted grid transitioning to an even grid for news and media
Bipolar comparison layout for rapid information retrieval
Objective data blocks to reinforce source-of-truth credibility
Why it mattered
Supported business goals without losing clarity
Helped users explore early and compare later
Reduced the need to leave the site to compare candidates
Balanced subjective campaign content with factual reference points
Designing mobile first helped ensure the hierarchy held up across every breakpoint.
What actually shipped
My designs didn't ship directly, but the bigger lesson was learning how editorial priorities, user needs, business timelines, and engineering constraints all shape the final product. I learned to present work, defend rationale, and not take iteration personally.
Smart TV banner

One banner in millions of living rooms.

I designed audience-focused messaging for CNN's Smart TV election banner by analyzing what would genuinely motivate action, not just inform. The copy strategy was simple: one clear header, one line of supporting text, a QR code, and a URL for accessibility.

Learnings

What high-stakes, high-speed design taught me.

01
Content design is systems design. Building guidelines wasn't just writing words. It was creating a system people could actually use.
02
Speed and quality aren't opposites if you have a system. Rapid iteration only worked because the work had a consistent structure underneath it.
03
The best copy is invisible. On the Smart TV banner, success meant a viewer didn't consciously read the copy. They just knew what to do next.
complexity is easy. clarity takes work.
