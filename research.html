Why this matters

As AI shifts from assistant to actor, trust becomes a design problem.

Traditional software waits for instructions. Agentic systems can plan, decide, and act across multiple steps with less human intervention. That shift changes what users need from an interface.

The challenge is no longer just whether an AI response is useful. It's whether people understand what the system is about to do, when to step in, and how trust should be repaired when something goes wrong.

Trust in agentic systems should be dynamic and collaborative, not one-sided. I wanted to explore what that means for the interfaces designers create.

My Role

An exploratory senior project grounded in real practitioner behavior.

I led this project independently from research question to presentation — understanding how people already work with AI today, where trust breaks down, and what interface patterns might better support human-agent collaboration in the future.

  • Framed the research question around trust in agentic systems
  • Conducted semi-structured interviews with 8 practitioners
  • Synthesized themes across behaviors, needs, and friction points
  • Translated findings into a product-facing framework
  • Presented the work publicly at NCUR 2026
Who informed it
UX designers and researchers using AI in daily workflows — spanning enterprise, independent, and student contexts, connected to organizations like Cisco, AWS, JPMC, and Visa.
What shaped my lens
My own experience designing AI-driven features during my Cisco internship — where I kept seeing that friction wasn't about model capability, but whether users understood the system well enough to trust it.

Insights

Three themes stood out across the research.

People didn't want blind automation. They wanted AI to accelerate work while still preserving accountability, clarity, and room for intervention.

01
AI is useful for low-risk acceleration, not blind autonomy.
Participants described AI as a productivity amplifier. It worked best when helping them move faster — not when taking over end-to-end responsibility without oversight.
02
People want reasoning, sources, and confidence signals.
Trust increased when the system exposed how it arrived at an answer, what information it relied on, and where uncertainty still existed.
03
Repeated prompting often signals low user confidence.
When people keep rephrasing or refining prompts, it often means the system hasn't interpreted intent well. Most tools treat this as input variation rather than a trust signal.

Framework

From findings to a bidirectional trust cycle.

Rather than designing for trust as something a human simply grants or withholds, I reframed trust as something built between both sides of the interaction. The result was a four-part framework for human-agent teaming.

Phase 1
Agent Planning
Before acting, the agent should clarify assumptions, define task boundaries, and surface ambiguity early. This helps prevent miscalibrated trust before it starts.
"I'm assuming these bold headers represent section titles. Please confirm before I restructure the document."
Phase 2
Agent Proposal
Instead of acting invisibly, the system should propose a path with rationale, sources, and confidence. Auditability becomes the foundation for accountability.
"Based on these three sources, I propose this outline. I couldn't independently verify one claim. Confidence: moderate."
Phase 3
Human Review
People need meaningful control, especially for higher-stakes actions. Clear review points, override paths, and visible changes keep authority where it matters most.
Before a report is sent, the system flags low-confidence sections, shows all edits, and requires explicit approval.
Phase 4
Mutual Learning
Corrections shouldn't disappear after a single interaction. The system should adapt from feedback, while also recognizing when repeated prompting reflects uncertainty or unmet intent.
After repeated rejections of formal copy, the agent learns the user prefers warmer language and asks about tone earlier next time.

Practical Applications

Translating the framework into concrete product concepts.

This research was never meant to stay theoretical. I'm using it as a foundation for interface explorations that show how trust, oversight, and adaptation can appear inside real product experiences.

Scroll to see all concepts →
Concept 01 · In design progress
Research Assistant Agent
An agent that helps gather sources, summarize findings, and draft structured outputs while making its reasoning visible before anything is finalized.
  • Clarifies the research goal and scope before beginning
  • Shows sources, traceability, and confidence beside key claims
  • Proposes an outline before generating the full deliverable
  • Requires human approval before a final summary is exported
Concept 02 · In design progress
Personal Workflow Agent
A planning companion that helps organize tasks, prioritize time, and suggest next steps without acting invisibly or taking over too much control.
  • Suggests actions before scheduling or committing changes
  • Surfaces why a task was prioritized and what signals informed it
  • Lets the user edit, reject, or fine-tune recommendations
  • Adapts over time based on preferences the user can see and control
Concept 03 · In design progress
Financial Copilot
A higher-stakes concept where the framework matters even more — the system proposes next steps, explains its logic, and preserves human authority where risk is involved.
  • Never auto-executes sensitive actions without explicit review
  • Shows rationale, exceptions, and low-confidence areas before approval
  • Creates a visible audit trail for actions, edits, and overrides
  • Supports trust repair by surfacing what changed after an error
Concept 04 · In design progress
Design Partner Agent
A concept focused on creative collaboration, where the system becomes more helpful by learning tone, preferences, and feedback patterns instead of just generating faster outputs.
  • Explains why each suggestion was made, not just what changed
  • Uses preference memory that can be reviewed, edited, or reset
  • Detects repeated revisions as a confidence signal
  • Gradually becomes a better collaborator through explicit and implicit feedback

Reflection

This project changed how I think about designing for AI systems.

Before this work, I was already interested in AI as a design space. But researching human-agent teaming pushed me further — thinking less about single outputs and more about the relationship people build with a system over time.

My experience designing AI-powered features at Cisco helped shape this perspective. I kept seeing that the friction wasn't about model capability. Often, it was about whether users understood the system well enough to trust it appropriately.

How do we design AI that feels capable without becoming opaque, and adaptive without becoming overbearing?

Future Work

This started as a senior project. I see it as the beginning of a longer design investigation.

I'm continuing to build on this work — exploring more practical industry use cases, translating the framework into clearer interface patterns, and testing what trustworthy human-agent collaboration could look like in everyday products.

Areas I'm exploring
  • Oversight without unnecessary friction
  • Trust repair after AI mistakes
  • Shared memory between people and agents
  • Legible autonomy in higher-stakes systems
What I'm building next

  • More applied explorations across industries
What I want to contribute

  • A more human-centered future for agentic systems
