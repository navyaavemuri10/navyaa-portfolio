Context

Employees had transportation data, but not transportation confidence.

Before the platform, a simple commute decision required employees to jump between Tesla One, parking availability, shuttle schedules, native maps, and transportation guidance. Even after checking availability, employees could arrive to find a lot full because the information changed before they got there.

Reframing the problem
The product was not just about showing parking availability. It was about helping employees decide the best way to get to work before they were already in transit.
Why it was challenging

The product sat at the intersection of changing data, ambitious stakeholders, and mobile constraints.

01 · Complex information
Employees needed to evaluate current availability, predicted occupancy, arrival time, shuttle options, and commute duration without feeling overwhelmed.
02 · Stakeholder ambition
Transportation stakeholders wanted predictive experiences and operational tooling, but the design had to stay feasible within engineering and data constraints.
03 · Mobile-first decisions
Commuters were checking information quickly and often on the go, which made hierarchy, glanceability, and next actions especially important.
Key tradeoff · Raw data vs decision confidence
Early concepts focused on parking counts and real-time availability. But employees did not only need to know what was available now — they needed to know whether it would still be available by arrival. This pushed the design toward predictive occupancy signals and alternate route recommendations.

Mobile constraints

Mobile design forced sharper choices about hierarchy, states, and accessibility.

This project helped me understand that mobile enterprise design is not just about fitting desktop information into a smaller space. It requires deciding what a user needs first, what can wait, and what must be accessible at a glance.

Glanceable
Lead with status and recommendation before exposing detailed lot or shuttle data.
Progressive
Reveal comparisons, alternatives, and operational details only when they help the next decision.
Thumb-first
Prioritize primary actions and route-switching patterns that are easy to trigger quickly.
State-aware
Design for full, limited, unknown, loading, and error states so the system remains useful when data changes.

Accessibility mattered because status could not rely on color alone.

Parking availability and prediction states needed clear labels, contrast, and redundant cues so employees could understand urgency quickly and confidently.

Final solution

A unified mobility platform embedded in Tesla One.

Impact & reflection

This project taught me that enterprise UX is often about designing confidence, not just screens.

The platform consolidated 4 disconnected transportation tools into one mobility workflow for 1,000+ employees, while giving parking managers clearer operational visibility. More importantly, it changed the product goal from exposing transportation data to supporting better decisions before employees arrived.

01
I learned to design for uncertainty. Real-time data alone was not enough. The most useful experience helped users understand what was likely to happen next.
02
I learned to balance ambition with feasibility. Strong enterprise design meant translating stakeholder requests into experiences that were useful, buildable, and clear.
03
I grew as a mobile designer. Designing for quick decisions on small screens forced me to be more disciplined with hierarchy, states, and next actions.
