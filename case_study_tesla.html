Context

Employees had transportation data, but not transportation confidence.

Before the platform, a simple commute decision required employees to jump between Tesla One, parking availability, shuttle schedules, native maps, and transportation guidance. Even after checking availability, employees could arrive to find a lot full because the information changed before they got there.

The problem was not finding information. It was synthesizing changing information quickly enough to make a confident decision before leaving for work.

Why it was challenging

The product sat at the intersection of changing data, ambitious stakeholders, and mobile constraints.

01 · Complex information
Employees needed to evaluate current availability, predicted occupancy, arrival time, shuttle options, and commute duration without feeling overwhelmed.
02 · Stakeholder ambition
Transportation stakeholders wanted predictive experiences and operational tooling, but the design had to stay feasible within engineering and data constraints.
03 · Mobile-first decisions
Commuters were checking information quickly and often on the go, which made hierarchy, glanceability, and next actions especially important.
Key tradeoff · Raw data vs decision confidence
Early concepts focused on parking counts and real-time availability. But employees did not only need to know what was available now — they needed to know whether it would still be available by arrival. This pushed the design toward predictive occupancy signals and alternate route recommendations.
Design principles

I used three principles to move the product from information display to decision support.

Because the data could change while employees were still commuting, the product could not simply show a static answer. Each feature had to help users understand what was happening, what was likely to happen next, and what action made the most sense.

01
Surface the next best action
Lead with a recommendation or status summary before exposing detailed parking, shuttle, or routing data.
02
Reduce context switching
Bring parking, shuttle, navigation, and reporting into one flow so users do not have to assemble the commute story themselves.
03
Design for uncertainty
Represent changing conditions through prediction, confidence cues, and alternate options instead of implying certainty.
Product tradeoff · Build maps vs focus the product
We explored how much navigation should live inside the platform, but rebuilding a full maps experience would have duplicated mature functionality and increased engineering complexity. The stronger product decision was to focus on what Tesla needed most: helping employees decide when and where to park, then connecting them to navigation at the right moment.

Designing for quick decisions

Accessibility and mobile hierarchy had to make status understandable at a glance.

Employees might check transportation information while leaving home, walking to a shuttle, or already in transit. I focused the mobile experience on clear status labels, redundant cues, thumb-safe actions, and states that stayed useful even when availability changed.

Final solution

A unified mobility platform embedded in Tesla One, shown through the core decision moments.

Instead of asking employees to interpret transportation data across multiple systems, the final experience guided them through the commute decision: check status, understand prediction, compare alternatives, navigate, and give managers operational visibility.

Employee moment

Decision support
Shift from “what is available now?” to “what will be available when I arrive?”
Prediction helped employees account for changing conditions instead of relying on a static number that could become outdated mid-commute.
Alternate route
Surface shuttle options when parking becomes risky.
The product reduced dead ends by giving employees a clear next step when their first commute option was unlikely to work.
Operations
Give transportation managers visibility into utilization.
Managers could monitor parking availability, identify capacity issues, and generate reports from the same platform employees used to make commute decisions.
Impact & reflection

This project taught me that enterprise UX is often about designing confidence, not just screens.

Before Tesla, I thought enterprise UX was mostly about organizing complex workflows. This project taught me that great enterprise products do not simply expose information — they help people make confident decisions despite changing conditions and uncertainty.

The platform consolidated 4 disconnected transportation tools into one mobility workflow for 1,000+ employees, while giving parking managers clearer operational visibility. More importantly, it changed the product goal from exposing transportation data to helping employees make better decisions before they arrived.

01
I learned to design for uncertainty. Real-time data alone was not enough. The most useful experience helped users understand what was likely to happen next.
02
I learned to balance ambition with feasibility. Strong enterprise design meant translating stakeholder requests into experiences that were useful, buildable, and clear.
03
I grew as a mobile designer. Designing for quick decisions on small screens forced me to be more disciplined with hierarchy, states, and next actions.
