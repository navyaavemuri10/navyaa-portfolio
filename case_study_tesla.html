Context

Employees had transportation data, but not transportation confidence.

Before the platform, a simple commute decision required employees to jump between Tesla One, parking availability, shuttle schedules, native maps, and transportation guidance. Even after checking availability, employees could arrive to find a lot full because the information changed before they got there.

The problem was not finding information. It was synthesizing changing information quickly enough to make a confident decision before leaving for work.

User flow

Before designing screens, we had to define how the product should work.

As one of the designers on the project, I shaped the core employee flow with support from the broader design team. The flow went through multiple iterations, stakeholder reviews, and client calls to align employee needs, transportation operations, and engineering constraints before we moved into high-fidelity screens.

This artifact became the shared foundation for the product: how employees would check availability, understand prediction, compare options, navigate, and how managers would monitor the system behind the scenes.

Why it was challenging

The hard part was not showing transportation data. It was helping employees act before the data changed.

01

Changing conditions

Parking availability could change between checking the app and arriving on-site.

02

Multiple user groups

Employees needed quick recommendations; managers needed operational visibility.

03

Mobile decisions

Commuters needed glanceable guidance while leaving home, walking, or already in transit.

Key product shift

Early concepts focused on real-time parking counts. The stronger direction was predictive: help employees understand whether parking would still be available by the time they arrived.

Design principles

Each principle mapped directly to a feature decision.

The goal was not to add more transportation data to Tesla One. The goal was to make the data actionable. These principles helped evaluate each screen: does it reduce interpretation, support the next step, and stay useful when conditions change?

01 · Surface the next best action
Lead with the recommendation, then reveal the data.
Feature shown: parking status and recommended next step. Instead of making employees interpret occupancy, the screen tells them what option is most viable and why.
02 · Reduce context switching
Keep parking, shuttle, and navigation in one flow.
Feature shown: unified commute options. Employees should not have to leave the experience to compare parking availability, shuttle timing, and route decisions.
03 · Design for uncertainty
Show what is likely to happen, not only what is happening now.
Feature shown: predictive occupancy. Because availability could change mid-commute, the interface needed to communicate confidence, risk, and alternatives instead of implying certainty.
Product tradeoff

We considered how much navigation should live inside the platform, but rebuilding maps would duplicate mature functionality. The stronger decision was to focus engineering effort on what differentiated the product: helping employees decide when and where to commute.

Designing for quick decisions

The mobile UI had to make the right action obvious in seconds.

Employees could be leaving home, walking, or already in transit. I focused the mobile experience on clear status labels, redundant cues, thumb-safe actions, and states that stayed useful when availability changed.

How did the screen reduce uncertainty or context switching?

Screen 01

Employees could quickly evaluate lot status and understand the most viable option before leaving or while already in transit.
Screen 02
Prediction shifted the question from now to arrival.

When the first option was unlikely to work, the interface surfaced shuttle or alternate lot options so employees still had a clear next step.

Managers could monitor availability, identify capacity issues, and generate reports from the same platform employees used to make commute decisions.

Takeaway: The product did not just combine tools. It turned transportation data into a guided decision system for employees and managers.

Impact & reflection

This project taught me that enterprise UX is often about designing confidence, not just screens.

The platform consolidated 4 disconnected transportation tools into one mobility workflow for 1,000+ employees, while giving parking managers clearer operational visibility. More importantly, it changed the product goal from exposing transportation data to helping employees make better decisions before they arrived.

01
I learned to design for uncertainty. Real-time data alone was not enough. The most useful experience helped users understand what was likely to happen next.
02
I learned to balance ambition with feasibility. The best solution was not always building more technology; it was deciding where the product should invest effort.
03
I grew as a mobile designer. Designing for quick decisions on small screens forced me to be more disciplined with hierarchy, states, and next actions.
