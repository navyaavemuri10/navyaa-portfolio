The problem

The prompt said "travel." We built for the trip most people take every single day.

Every other team went for flights, hotels, or trip itineraries. We took a step back. The prompt asked for a travel experience that optimized for time, cost, and convenience. We asked ourselves: what travel problem affects people every day, not just when they book a vacation? For five women on our team, the answer came quickly. Walking alone, and the anxiety that comes with it.

We validated that instinct fast. The data confirmed it was not just a personal experience. It was a widespread, documented problem with no good product solution.

45%
of women feel unsafe taking walks in their area (Gallup, 2023)
60%
of adults felt unsafe walking alone after dark in open spaces (ONS, 2021)
40%
of Americans afraid to walk alone at night, highest level since 1993 (Gallup, 2025)
Research

50 data points. 3 rounds of affinity mapping. Under 4 hours.

We ran rapid user interviews and synthesized findings through three rounds of affinity mapping in FigJam. The goal was not to confirm our assumptions. It was to find what was actually true about how people experienced walking alone and what they were already doing to manage that anxiety.

↑ RESEARCH: FigJam screenshot of the affinity mapping session showing all interview data synthesized
3 core pain points
Route uncertainty: extra time spent researching unfamiliar areas before walking
Location sharing: informing trusted people for reassurance
Phone calls as safety behavior: real or simulated calls to deter threats
What existed and why it fell short
Google Maps: fastest route, not safest route
Waze: built for drivers, not pedestrians
Citizen: crime data, no route safety or navigation
Nothing addressed pedestrian route safety holistically
The hard call

We designed a feature and then cut it ourselves. Here is why.

Early in ideation, we designed a neighborhood safety rating system. Users could see which areas had higher or lower safety scores before planning their route. It felt like an obvious, useful feature. It directly addressed the route uncertainty pain point we had found in research.

The decision that mattered most
We designed a neighborhood safety rating feature and then cut it ourselves — not because it was hard to build, but because we could not build it without encoding bias into a product that vulnerable people would use to make real decisions.
The feature we built
Neighborhood safety ratings: users could see which areas scored higher or lower before routing. Directly addressed the route uncertainty pain point.
The problem we found
Ratings would reflect policing bias, not actual danger. Over-policed and predominantly non-white neighborhoods would appear more dangerous regardless of reality.
Team disagreement
Some of us felt the feature addressed a real user need. Cutting it meant losing something users had explicitly asked for.
What we built instead
Incident-based crowdsourcing with time-decay. Specific events, not neighborhood generalizations. Current conditions, not historical bias.
Ideation

Translating pain points into features. Then cutting the ones we could not build ethically.

With the safety ratings removed, we focused on three core features: crowdsourced incident reporting with time-decay, ETA sharing with trusted contacts, and an AI companion for high-anxiety walking moments powered by GPT-4o. I led the research into how GPT-4o actually worked before we committed to designing around it.

Key decisions from lo-fi and mid-fi testing
Three features went into testing. Two came out cut. One got a bigger investment. Here is why.
Cut: navigation bar
Users were trying to navigate the nav before they had oriented themselves in the app. Information overload from the first screen.
The fix
Removed entirely. Replaced with a single focused entry flow. Orient first, navigate second.
Cut: friends' route visibility
Every user we tested mentioned apps they already used for this. We were rebuilding something that existed.
The decision
Removed. Not a differentiator — feature duplication. Redirected that design time to what was actually unique about Navi.
Kept: AI companion call
Most technically ambitious feature. Real risk of over-scoping in a 24-hour window.
Why we kept it
Testing showed real anxiety reduction in walking simulations. The data justified the investment. We committed.

Design

Material Design as a foundation. Our design time went where it mattered.

With 24 hours total, we made a deliberate choice to base our visual system on Material Design. Users already understand it intuitively, its component library is well-documented, and using it let us move fast without sacrificing consistency. Our limited design time went entirely to the interactions that actually differentiated Navi: the route safety selection flow, the ETA sharing, and the AI companion call experience.

"Even short, real-time conversations with AI can increase user confidence and reduce anxiety when walking alone."

Outcome
🏆
First place, UX Fest SLO
Among 20 or more teams. Judges specifically cited our research depth and the AI companion feature as the strongest differentiators. The decision to cut neighborhood safety ratings also came up in the evaluation as a sign of mature, ethical design thinking.

What made Navi win was not the most features or the most polished visuals. It was the combination of real research done under extreme time pressure and hard decisions made on the basis of that research. The cut we made hurt us in the short term and helped us in ways that mattered.

Learnings

What 24 hours of pressure with a real ethical question in the middle teaches you.

01
Rapid research still gives you real, actionable insights. We had hours, not weeks. But even a quick round of user interviews and affinity mapping surfaced things we would never have caught on our own: the nav bar overload, the redundancy with existing apps, and the structural problems with neighborhood ratings. Research does not have to be long to change your decisions.
02
Validate the technology before designing around it. I led the research into GPT-4o capabilities and limitations before we committed to the AI companion feature. Designing around AI assumptions you have not tested produces features that look good in a prototype and fail in a demo. We tested first and designed second.
03
Designing for safety means designing against bias, not just toward comfort. The hardest moment in this project was cutting the neighborhood safety ratings. They addressed a real user need. They also risked encoding real harm into a product that vulnerable people would use to make real decisions. That tension, between what feels useful and what is actually responsible, is something I think about carefully on every project now.
